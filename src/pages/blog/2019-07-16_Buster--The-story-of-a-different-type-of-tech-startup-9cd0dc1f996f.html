Buster: The story of a different type of tech startup

My name is Holden.

This is the story of how I started a tech business, without following any of the popular advice.

Don’t expect this to be a polished novel. These are raw notes and thoughts as I go through day to day trying to run this business.

Who am I?

I grew up in the Midwest, but have been involved in technology since I was 17 years old.

I’ve worked in the heart of the Midwest, inside one of the largest companies in Silicon Valley, and at startups with funding.

I’ve worn a lot of different hats as an intern, a frontend developer, backend developer, full stack developer, infrastructure engineer, support person, business analyst, team leader, project manager, salesperson (essentially), and consultant.

Bad advice

People with my background are the prime candidates to join the Zuckerberg, Musk, or Bezos cults.

Unfortunately, I did for a while.

I tried to raise funding for about 5 different projects in college, without really building anything substantial because that’s some of the advice you get. Sell it first, raise money, build it later.

College really feels like a time where I spent years heading the wrong direction because of the heavy influence of the Facebook story.

No one will explain to you that companies like Facebook are in the 0.000001%. They just pretend that it’s the only way to run a company successfully.

So I went 100% in the opposite direction after realizing that there was another way for me, and for most people interested in starting their own business.

Maybe I will never run a billion dollar company this way, but I’m cool with that.

My type of business

For the same reason that I do not take advice from a financial adviser who recommends to quit your job, sell your house, and put 100% of your money into lottery tickets, I do not like to take business advice from folks who tell you to quit your job, sell your house, put 100% of your money into a long shot business that’s losing $10k’s of dollars each month, and then cross your fingers that one of the top 100 companies in the country will acquire you for billions of dollars.

Here are some of the traits describing what I want my business to look like:

  • Calm — The opposite of “move fast and break things”.
  • Nurturing — Provide an environment to develop talent instead of strictly poaching talent.
  • Profitable — Never be in debt.
  • Self Funded and Free — Do not be responsible for answering to any external stakeholders. Only be responsible for answering to employees of the business.
  • Patient — Think about the long term. Do not rush into anything.
  • Empathetic — Treat employees, customers, and vendors like human beings that you care for. Give them the proper wages, benefits, and environment.
  • Organic — Develop the company and products organically without over analysis. This means giving up many metrics, reports, forecasts, and plans that most companies would slave over.
  • Useful — The business should only exist if it’s providing useful services to the world. It doesn’t need to dominate the marketplace, or “crush” its competition. It just needs to be useful to a subset of the population, in some sector.
  • Self Focused — Only worried about itself, its employees, its customers, and its vendors.

Today is July 15th, 2019.

This whole thing started a few years ago, so you’ve already missed quite a bit of me fumbling around in different directions.

The business is called Buster Technologies and was registered back in 2016 because I built a side project that I wanted to use at my full-time job.

It was an app to help us get up and move around (software developers can get fat after a few years of sitting at their desks 8 hours a day.)

Since then I’ve introduced 2 more products underneath the same umbrella. The first 2 make no money. I spent a lot of time on them, but I convinced myself they weren’t the right direction. Maybe for better or for worse.

The 3rd one stuck pretty well. I now spend the majority of my (free) time on it. It’s profitable and has a few happy customers.

Working in my free time

So like you saw above, I work in my free time.

Yes, I still have a full-time job developing software for a company, which is not my own.

People who are avid watchers of Shark Tank, and now think they are master investors, probably would freak out to hear this.

“You gotta go all in”

“There’s not enough time in the day”

“I can’t afford the resources to start a business without funding”

These are all quotes that have been directed at me at some point from random people, people I’ve asked for advice or people who have asked me for advice.

And maybe they apply for some people. There’s no silver bullet. Some people out there really don’t have time and have to quit their jobs. Some people can’t get to the mindset of running a side business while working full time because of stress or personality.

But I think everyone needs to consider it as an option. It’s just another one of the tools in an entrepreneurs belt when starting a business.

The bootstrapped side business

That’s where I’m at:

5–10 hours a week

I have averaged about 5–10 hours a week for the last 3 years. That’s sometimes 1 long Sunday or a couple of hours a day throughout the week.

No funding

I have received $0 in outside help. Right now, I spend about $100 a month in expenses, and that could be cut down a lot more if I had to.

I spend the money mainly on Google Apps and servers from Digital Ocean to run my applications.

Writing a lot

This is something that did not come naturally to me and still can be tough at times.

When running this sort of side project, I don’t have too much money for marketing. No money at first.

So I write a lot more than I used to. I write blog posts on medium, I write on a company blog, I write on Linkedin, and I reach out to people via Twitter.

A few users a month

At times, things are growing slow. Sometimes it’s only a few users a month. And that’s perfectly fine.

I’m just doing my best to keep trust in the product and focus on the paying customers' feedback/happiness.

Not hiring until it hurts

This is a concept I’ve learned from the founders of Basecamp.

With my business, I’ve been able to bring thousands of extra income for myself, by myself at first. I have no employees and hire out certain tasks as needed. But managing even a few hundred customers on my own is doable despite being stressful at times.

If I get to the point where I have 1,000 customers, generating $500k+ a year in profit, and I start to feel too much pressure then I may need to hire a one or two employees, but I won’t hire just because I have the cash to do so.

Talking to other business owners

I’m also talking to other people in my position. I have a couple of business owners who I message nearly every other day just to talk through what’s going on. I’ve learned a ton and been able to stay a little more motivated just by hearing some other stories.
