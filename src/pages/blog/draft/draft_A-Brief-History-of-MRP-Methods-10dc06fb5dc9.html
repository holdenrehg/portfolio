A Brief History of MRP Methods

Explanation of MRP and other planning methodologies

I write software for a living.

So I am not formally trained on manufacturing technologies. I’m not a mechanical engineer. I’m not a process engineer. I don’t use SolidWorks. I don’t design or manage supply chains.

But I do deal with a lot of manufacturing software and have designed software systems for small to medium manufacturers. Over the course of the last 5 years or so, I’ve run into quite a few different methodologies (some old and some new) that I’ve been forced to learn.

I want to explain what I’ve learned.

If you are heavily involved in the industry, then you might already be familiar with most of what I’m going to describe. For everyone like myself, who were not trained in these manufacturing or supply chain roles, I’m going to try to explain the major technologies, philosophies, and methodologies that started with MRP in the 1950s and has led up to some of the most recent methods like DDMRP in the 2010s.


Short Description

Before we jump into a timeline of events, let’s quickly define MRP. The formal definition of MRP (according to Wikipedia) says:

Material requirements planning (MRP) is a production planning, scheduling, and inventory control system used to manage manufacturing processes.

This is a definitely modern definition. At the core of MRP, I would describe it as:

Material requirement planning (MRP) is ensuring that parts and products are where you need them when you need them while relying on minimal amounts of on hand inventory.

Over time MRP has changed definitions because has gone through different iterations. I’ll describe those iterations in the following sections, but remember in the end it’s about supply chain and getting parts/products moved around a business.
